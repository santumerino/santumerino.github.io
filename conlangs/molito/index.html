pamoto Ta:Palo:Poloti:Katamoti:Molito Molito:Poloti:Patomo Pi:Palo:Poloti:Mataka lotimo

(“Speech intended for people to engage other’s brains via their ears”)

Overview

pamoto Ta:Palo:Poloti:Katamoti:Molito Molito:Poloti:Patomo Pi:Palo:Poloti:Mataka lotimo is a philosophical and artistic language with a small vocabulary, a totally culturally neutral approach, all while having the same discursive ability as any natural language (probably)! It is designed to be minimalist, but not excessively so: 32 words oughta be enough for anyone.

Goals

As mentioned earlier, pamoto Ta:Palo:Poloti:Katamoti:Molito Molito:Poloti:Patomo Pi:Palo:Poloti:Mataka lotimo strives for cultural neutrality. Of course, to truly get rid of one’s cultural baggage is impossible, even if one is aware of it, and token gestures like adopting a foreign culture’s superficial elements without deep consideration and respect of their origins risks exoticisation, cultural appropriation, and breeding “noble savage” narratives. However, recognising that one is inherently biased is half the battle.
This is why pamoto Ta:Palo:Poloti:Katamoti:Molito Molito:Poloti:Patomo Pi:Palo:Poloti:Mataka lotimo’s phonotactics are meant to minimise tricky cross-language distinctions, leading directly to its significantly reduced phonetic inventory and strict CV structure.

pamoto Ta:Palo:Poloti:Katamoti:Molito Molito:Poloti:Patomo Pi:Palo:Poloti:Mataka lotimo is also meant to simplify thought and expression, which, needless to say, is extremely desirable. Opaque terminology is completely anathema to this project: just say what you mean, context is disambiguation enough!
Because of this, even though pamoto Ta:Palo:Poloti:Katamoti:Molito Molito:Poloti:Patomo Pi:Palo:Poloti:Mataka lotimo is obviously a living language, coinages and lexicalisation are strongly discouraged. Again, context is disambiguation enough! Think of it as a more primitive language a hunter-gatherer society would use, which, of course, has zero troubling implications and is by no means a case of exoticisation or of creating a “noble savage” narrative.

Finally, this language is meant to be functional. Everything that natural languages can express is able to be expressed in pamoto Ta:Palo:Poloti:Katamoti:Molito Molito:Poloti:Patomo Pi:Palo:Poloti:Mataka lotimo, if you try hard enough. It’s only a matter of thinking about what you want to say, remembering the vocabulary, remembering the grammar rules, remembering that people might misinterpret you and you’ll have to restate what you mean (if they even recognise they misunderstood you, since any one sentence can have several valid translations), and remembering to have fun :)

Phonotactics

All syllables in pamoto Ta:Palo:Poloti:Katamoti:Molito Molito:Poloti:Patomo Pi:Palo:Poloti:Mataka lotimo are strictly CV, and the sequences /mi/ and /la/ are forbidden. The maximum length for a word is three syllables (with the exception of Kilatatamakalo; see the Vocabulary section).

Grammar

pamoto Ta:Palo:Poloti:Katamoti:Molito Molito:Poloti:Patomo Pi:Palo:Poloti:Mataka lotimo is an SOV language. To be clear, that means that the only possible structure is SOV. Other languages have predominant or “default” orders, pamoto Ta:Palo:Poloti:Katamoti:Molito Molito:Poloti:Patomo Pi:Palo:Poloti:Mataka lotimo has just the one. Minimalism!

Any given word can belong to any part of speech, with its position in the sentence being the biggest clue as to what it might be.

Nouns and pronouns

When a word is used as a noun, it might be used by itself without being marked for number, in which case it is ambiguous. Should one want to clarify whether it is a singular or a plural, one might append the words Limo or Molo, respectively.

Verbs

When a word is used as a verb, it might be used by itself without any explicit temporal reference, in which case context is key to disambiguate. Should you want to specify when an event happens, appending words such as Patito (for present actions), Lotimo (for future actions) or Tite (for past actions) is a valid strategy.

About objects

As mentioned earlier, pamoto Ta:Palo:Poloti:Katamoti:Molito Molito:Poloti:Patomo Pi:Palo:Poloti:Mataka lotimo is strictly SOV. To be more specific, the direct object is placed in second position, and the information which would be carried by an indirect object is either to be a modifier to the verb or a prepositional phrase (as will be mentioned below), depending on the intended meaning and issues of information density.

Modifiers

Modifiers are highly useful for clarifying meaning. They are postpended to the word they modify (see the Orthography section for information on how to represent this in writing).

A theoretically unlimited number of modifiers might be attached to a word. These form a modifier phrase, all of which affects the word they’re attached to as a unit. That is to say, Palo:Poloti:Katamoti:Molito is a “noise-perception-capable organ” (an ear), not a “noisy perception-capable organ” or what have you.

Prepositions

Prepositions and prepositional phrases, considered distinct from modifiers, are placed at the beginning of the sentence. It is not the same to say Li Makima:Molito katamoti:mataka (“Xe imagines a noisy animal”) than to say mataka Li Makima:Molito katamoti (“Xe hears the noisy animal thoughtfully”).

Clauses

Unlike every natural language (and most constructed languages), pamoto Ta:Palo:Poloti:Katamoti:Molito Molito:Poloti:Patomo Pi:Palo:Poloti:Mataka lotimo features no recursive clause structures.

Whereas a language like English might be able to form structures like “He told me that they went to the park”, pamoto Ta:Palo:Poloti:Katamoti:Molito Molito:Poloti:Patomo Pi:Palo:Poloti:Mataka lotimo will instead form a structure like “He told me something. That something was about them. They went somewhere. That somewhere is the park.” (or, in the language, Pi Patito molito:ma. Pi Patito:Pamoto:Ta molito. Ta tite Patito. Patito Patito:Tapilo tite.)

Orthography

Sentences in pamoto Ta:Palo:Poloti:Katamoti:Molito Molito:Poloti:Patomo Pi:Palo:Poloti:Mataka lotimo need not start with a capital letter. The initial letter in nouns is always capitalised, and modifiers match the capitalisation of whichever word they are attached to, so it might be the case that a sentence does start with a capital letter, but should it begin with a prepositional phrase, it is best to leave the first letter un-capitalised to reduce ambiguity.

Vocabulary

pamoto Ta:Palo:Poloti:Katamoti:Molito Molito:Poloti:Patomo Pi:Palo:Poloti:Mataka lotimo’s vocabulary is largely borrowed from non-European sources, with only some European roots, and a few a priori words. Why this should be at all relevant, considering that borrowed words are so distorted as to be practically a priori anyway (and, when recognisable, so semantically shifted as to be actively detrimental to understanding), is left as an exercise to the reader.
Needless to say, because of the reduced vocabulary, words don’t have strict meanings so much as wide semantic spaces they refer to. Combining several words to restrict their referent is not only encouraged, but mandatory in most circumstances.

On loanwords

It might be the case that a word not from pamoto Ta:Palo:Poloti:Katamoti:Molito Molito:Poloti:Patomo Pi:Palo:Poloti:Mataka lotimo might need to be used in the language. This could happen when referring to someone by name, for example.
pamoto Ta:Palo:Poloti:Katamoti:Molito Molito:Poloti:Patomo Pi:Palo:Poloti:Mataka lotimo allows for loanwords to be directly copied from the language: when it writing, this is as simple as spelling the word as one would in said language (such that a proper name like Ἰσκαριώτης is represented as such). In spoken language, speakers of pamoto Ta:Palo:Poloti:Katamoti:Molito Molito:Poloti:Patomo Pi:Palo:Poloti:Mataka lotimo might naturally be inclined to apply their phonotactic rules (such that this name might be pronounced something like /’likali ’lopi/).

Some speakers might be tempted to rewrite the word to better fit the spoken language. This is not considered a valid strategy (such that *Likali Lopi or similar is not a valid way of representing Ἰσκαριώτης), at it may cause confusion as to what is a pamoto Ta:Palo:Poloti:Katamoti:Molito Molito:Poloti:Patomo Pi:Palo:Poloti:Mataka lotimo word and what isn’t. Remember, this language can represent anything natural languages can, so inventing new words is unnecessary!
