The Costume

Castellano - English

"NR/DK"

— Abbreviation used in surveys

It has many names, many of which I dare not pronounce, much less write, so as to not expose something that has already been very well exposed nor affirm something which I am still not certain of and which, I must say, I am scared to confirm to be true.

I don’t know when it became a part of me, when it infected me with answer-less questions, but I’ll venture to give out a number: thirteen years old. At thirteen years old, I already felt “weird”, if one can claim that word to have any meaning. Let me be more specific: I felt at the mercy of people, peers, mates, who seemed to live in a world I was unaware of, or one for which I had never been given a map, and the only way to navigate it was by looking at what they made me do, what they did to me, and guess whether the path was here or there.

It was then that I first became aware of the stowaway. And, as soon as we first looked at each other, she, he, lunged at me. It told me its name, a name, and it pointed out something I had never noticed before; the others were wearing a costume! But of course! How was I not going to feel “weird” if I was the only one without a costume?

Very deftly, it produced a costume from somewhere and gave it to me. It was too small for me, it itched, it looked horrible, clown-like. I asked why the others didn’t seem like they found the costume uncomfortable, if I was the only one for whom it was itchy, for whom it was small, horrible, clown-like. It laughed, and started to leave. I stretched my arm towards the stowaway, trying to grab it.

I didn’t manage to reach it.
Five years passed.

I must have been some eighteen years old when it appeared again. At that point, I had already grown to like the costume a bit, even if it sometimes itched and was sometimes too small. People recognised me with the costume on, and if I feigned to take it off, even for a second, they looked at me weirdly. Well, not everyone. By that time I had met people who wore other costumes, or who wore the same costume, but with a patch here or a stitch there, and who did not seem to care if I feigned to take it off. It was then that it appeared again. It told me its name, a different name than last time, and I asked it if I could also change or change out of the costume.

Very deftly, it reminded me that I had grown to like the costume a bit, and that people recognised me with the costume on. What would it mean to change out of the costume, or to change it? Was it worth the risk of ruining it, of staining it? I told it it was right, at that point I had grown accustomed to the costume, but why didn’t it tell me, back then, that there were more costumes to choose from? It laughed, and started to leave. I waved my arms as I kept asking it, over and over.

I didn’t manage to reach it.
Five years passed.

Now, I’m twenty-three. I was able to anticipate its approach because I had the costume on and, when it starts itching, it’s because it’s nearby. It started to tell me its name, another name, but I interrupted. I told it enough was enough, that I found out that not only was my costume not the only one, not only were there different models, bigger sizes, fabrics that itched less, but that I didn’t even need to wear a costume in the first place. I told it that I became aware of people who were like me when I was thirteen, without a costume, who reached adulthood costume-less and were happy. I told it that the costume was only useful to me if I wanted to act in the same play as everyone else, but that what everyone else did did not matter to me and the play did not appeal to me.

Very deftly, it retorted that, if that were true, why did I still have the costume on? Why did I take on that role every day, a poor player that struts and frets his hour upon the stage, if the play did not matter to me and did not appeal to me? It told me to stop being a hypocrite, that if I had not changed out of the costume at that point it was not its problem. It told me to stop being a crybaby, that I was going to stain the costume. It laughed, and started to leave.

But this time, I reached it. I ran and I lunged at them and I grabbed the bastard by the neck and I yelled, I yelled, I yelled why were they doing this to me, why did they give me a costume that itched, why did they give me useless solutions and then laugh, why did they always leave me with the same questions and no answers, why did they have many names, why did they not tell me what their real fucking name is, if they want to act so tough.

It whispered a name. I have no idea if it was the real one. Once again, it gave no response. Once again, I do not know. But in its voice I felt, for the first time, an emotion.

I let it go, and it returned to the same place it has stowed away in since five years ago, and since five years before that. Today, I still wear the costume so as to not expose something that has already been very well exposed nor affirm something which I am still not certain of and which, I must say, I am scared to confirm to be true.
